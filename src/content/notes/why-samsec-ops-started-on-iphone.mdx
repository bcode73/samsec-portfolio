The most common question about SamSec Ops, once people understand what it actually does, is some version of
"why is an SSH and infrastructure tool a phone app?" It's a fair question. Most infrastructure work happens at
a desk, in front of a real keyboard, with a terminal already open. A phone seems like an odd place to start.

The short answer: it wasn't chosen because a phone is the best interface for this work. It was chosen because
it was the fastest path to shipping something real, well-built, and narrow enough to actually finish.

## The constraint that mattered most

With one person building this, "build for iOS, Android, desktop, and web at once" wasn't a serious option. It
sounds appealing on a roadmap slide and falls apart the moment you try to make any single platform actually
good. Spreading the same effort across four platforms doesn't produce four decent apps, it produces one
mediocre app wearing four different skins.

So the real decision wasn't "mobile vs. desktop" in the abstract. It was "which one platform, built well, is
worth shipping first." That's a much narrower and more answerable question.

## What iOS specifically got right for free

A few things tipped it toward iOS rather than being an arbitrary starting point:

**Face ID as a session-protection primitive.** [SSH sessions in SamSec Ops stay protected by Face ID](/samsec-ops#capabilities)
without interrupting an already-connected session. That's not something you build from scratch, it's a
platform capability iOS already provides well. Building the equivalent trust model on a different platform
from zero would have been real, unglamorous security engineering before a single line of the actual product
got built.

**A consistent hardware and OS baseline.** iOS devices are a much narrower target than "Android," which spans
wildly different hardware, OS versions, and manufacturer customizations. That consistency meant less time
spent on device-specific edge cases and more time on the actual terminal, AI assistant, and agent logic.

**Real distribution.** The App Store is an existing, trusted distribution channel. A self-hosted web tool
would have needed to build trust and discovery from nothing.

None of that means the other platforms are wrong choices, permanently. It means they weren't the fastest way
to find out if the core idea, a real SSH terminal plus a context-aware AI assistant plus safe autonomous
execution, actually held up as a product people wanted to use daily.

## What this ruled out, on purpose

Committing to iOS-first meant explicitly not building Desktop, Web, and Android in parallel. That's a
real trade-off, not a free lunch. Engineers whose primary workflow is a Linux desktop don't have this tool
available to them yet, and that's a legitimate limitation, not a footnote. It's listed plainly as future
platform work on [the roadmap](/samsec-ops#roadmap), rather than implied to already exist somewhere.

<Callout type="tip">
Scoping a v1 tightly enough that it can actually ship well is its own kind of discipline. The temptation to
hedge by building "a little bit of everything" usually produces the worst version of all of it.
</Callout>

## The part that surprised me

Building for a phone first ended up shaping decisions that will likely outlast the platform choice itself.
Because a phone screen has no room for a sprawling interface, the SSH terminal, the AI assistant, and
SamSec Agent all had to justify their place in a tight space. That pressure is a large part of why the
authorization model behind SamSec Agent, [plan first, classify risk, confirm explicitly](/adr/adr-002-human-authorization),
is as deliberately minimal as it is. There wasn't room to bolt on a complicated permissions system, so it had
to be simple enough to actually fit, and simple enough to actually be understood by the person using it.

That's written up in more detail as [ADR-001: Why SamSec Ops Launched on iPhone First](/adr/adr-001-iphone-first),
if you want the fuller reasoning and the alternatives that were considered and set aside.
